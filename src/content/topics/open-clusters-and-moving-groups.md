Stars are rarely born alone. They usually form in large clouds of gas and dust
that collapse and fragment into dozens, hundreds, or even thousands of
individual stars. These newborn stars are initially held together by gravity
in "open clusters."

Over time, these clusters slowly dissolve as the stars drift away from each other.
However, they still share a common origin, a common chemical composition,
and a common motion through the galaxy. These loose collections are called
"moving groups."

By measuring the precise positions and velocities of stars, Gaia has revealed
many hidden families of stars that were previously thought to be unrelated.
Finding these groups helps astronomers piece together the history of star
formation in the Milky Way and the movements of our galaxy's disc.

clue to their common birth.
