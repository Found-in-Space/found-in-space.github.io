When a star like our Sun runs out of fuel, it doesn't just disappear. Instead, it
expels its outer layers (forming a planetary nebula) and leaves behind its
extremely dense, hot core. This core is a white dwarf.

White dwarfs are roughly the size of the Earth, but they have the mass of a full
Sun. This means their density is almost unimaginable: a single teaspoon of
white dwarf material would weigh as much as a mountain.

Because they are no longer undergoing fusion, white dwarfs are essentially "dead"
stars. They are simply cooling down over billions of years. In the nearby solar
neighbourhood, many of the faintest objects we can detect are these cooling
corpses of ancient stars.

<p class="panel__eyebrow">The Future of the Sun</p>
<h3>Our Sun's Ultimate Destiny</h3>
<p>
In about five billion years, our own Sun will become a white dwarf. It will
slowly cool over many billions more years until it eventually becomes a cold,
black dwarf—a fate it will share with most of the stars in the Milky Way.
