If you could see every star in the galaxy, you would find that most of them are
faint and red. These "red dwarfs" (technically M-dwarfs) account for about 75%
of all the stars in the Milky Way.

Red dwarfs are much smaller than our Sun, with masses between 8% and 50% of
the solar mass. Because they are so small and have low surface temperatures,
they are extremely faint. Not a single red dwarf is visible to the naked eye
from Earth—even Proxima Centauri, our closest stellar neighbour.

But what they lack in brightness, they make up for in longevity. Red dwarfs burn
their fuel so slowly that they can live for trillions of years, which is
longer than the current age of the universe.

<h3>A Faint Neighbourhood</h3>
<p>
Of the roughly 60 stars within 15 light-years of the Sun, about 50 are red
dwarfs. This means our local solar neighbourhood is populated mostly by stars
we cannot see with the naked eye.
