In astronomy, magnitude is the measure of a star's brightness. Apparent magnitude
is how bright a star looks to us on Earth. This depends on both the star's real
brightness (luminosity) and its distance from us.

A star can look very bright just because it is extremely close (like Sirius),
even if it isn't unusually luminous. Conversely, a very luminous star can look
faint if it is far away (like Rigel).

To compare stars fairly, astronomers use absolute magnitude. This is the magnitude
a star would have if it were placed at a standard distance of 10 parsecs
(about 32.6 light-years). Absolute magnitude measures a star's intrinsic luminosity.

<h3>Brightness drops quickly</h3>
<p>
As you move away from a star, its apparent brightness drops off with the square
of the distance. Double the distance, and the star becomes four times fainter.
This is why distance measurement is so crucial for understanding the real
nature of the stars we see.
