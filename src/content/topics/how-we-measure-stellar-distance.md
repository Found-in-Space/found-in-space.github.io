The most reliable way to measure the distance to a nearby star is using
trigonometric parallax. As Earth orbits the Sun, our viewpoint changes by 300 million
kilometres every six months.

From these two opposite points in our orbit, a nearby star will appear to shift
its position against much more distant background stars. By measuring that tiny
angle of shift—the parallax—we can calculate the distance to the star using
simple high-school trigonometry.

The challenge is that the angles are incredibly small. The largest parallax shift
ever measured is for Proxima Centauri, and it is less than one arcsecond—equivalent
to the width of a human hair seen from 20 kilometres away.
