The "main sequence" is the most stable phase of a star's life. During this phase,
the star is burning hydrogen into helium in its core through nuclear fusion.
This provides the outward pressure that balances the inward pull of gravity.

About 90% of the stars in the galaxy are on the main sequence. For these stars,
there is a simple relationship: mass determines everything. A more massive star
burns hotter (and therefore bluer) and more brightly than a lower-mass star.

Our Sun is a main-sequence star. It has been in this stable phase for about
five billion years, and it will remain here for another five billion years
before its hydrogen core is exhausted.

<div class="callout panel">
<p class="panel__eyebrow">A Vital Connection</p>
<h3>Mass is Destiny</h3>
<p>
Once a star enters the main sequence, its mass decides how long it will live,
what colour it will have, and how it will eventually die. The most massive
stars burn through their fuel in a few million years, while the smallest
ones will outlast the current age of the universe.
