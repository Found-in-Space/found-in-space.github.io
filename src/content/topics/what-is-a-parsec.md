The word "parsec" is short for "parallax second." It is a unit of distance based
directly on geometry. One parsec is the distance at which a star's parallax angle
is exactly one arcsecond.

In terms of light-years, one parsec is about 3.26. While light-years are easier to
talk about in everyday language, parsecs are the natural unit for mapping stars.
This is because we measure the distance of nearby stars using geometry, not time.

To give you an idea of scale: the nearest star (Proxima Centauri) is 1.3 parsecs
away. The Galactic Center is about 8,000 parsecs away.

<h3>Why astronomers use parsecs</h3>
<p>
Astrometry—the science of measuring where things are in space—starts with the
parallax shift. Since that shift is measured in arcseconds, the parsec is the
unit that comes directly from the measurement itself.
