When we look up at night, the sky feels like a single, distant canopy. The stars of
Orion or the Big Dipper seem to belong together because they form a recognisable
pattern on that flat surface. In reality, that flatness is an illusion of perspective.

A constellation is a human-made grouping. If you were to move even a few dozen
light-years away from Earth, the stars that make up Orion would begin to drift apart.
Some stars are "nearby" (within 20–50 light-years), while others in the same pattern
might be hundreds or thousands of light-years further away.

To truly understand the galaxy, we have to stop seeing the sky as a map on a sphere and
start seeing it as a volume of space. This shift from 2D to 3D is the core of modern
astrometry.

<p class="panel__eyebrow">Key Idea</p>
<h3>Perspective is everything</h3>
<p>
If you could view the Solar System from the side, you would see that the "flat"
night sky is actually a deep corridor of stars, each at a vastly different stage of
life and distance from us.
